long hours online.
This project looks at the relationship between social media use and
mental health symptoms.
We want to understand what's actually happening not just guess or blame the

Social media detox and youth mental health
AFFILIATION AND AUDIENCE: ANALYZING THE IMPACT OF DIGITAL BEHAVIOR ON THE
MENTAL HEALTH OF YOUNG PEOPLE IN THE AGE GROUP (18-24 YEARS).
METHODOLOGY: USING A "DIGITAL PHENOTYPE" AND "EMA" TO ACCURATELY TRACK
ACTUAL BEHAVIOR.
EXPERIMENT: REDUCING SOCIAL MEDIA USE TO JUST 30 MINUTES PER DAY.
DURATION OF EFFECT: SIGNIFICANT IMPROVEMENT IN MENTAL WELL-BEING WAS
OBSERVED WITHIN JUST ONE WEEK.
RESULTS: A MARKED REDUCTION IN SYMPTOMS OF ANXIETY, DEPRESSION, AND SLEEP
DISTURBANCES.

CONCLUSION
Social media usage shows clear links to mental well-being, with higher screen time
associated with increased anxiety, emotional fatigue, irritability, and reduced sleep
quality. Certain behaviors, like frequent morning checking, doom scrolling, and
consuming short-form content, appear to intensify these effects, while more moderate
or purposeful use tends to be less harmful. Overall, the findings suggest that how and
how much people engage with social media plays a critical role in their daily mood and
health. To improve well-being, it is advisable to set screen time limits, avoid phone use
immediately after waking or before sleep, and prioritize more meaningful or educational
content over passive scrolling. Small habit changes, such as taking regular digital breaks
and being mindful of usage patterns, can make a significant difference in maintaining a
healthier balance.
