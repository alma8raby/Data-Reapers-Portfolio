Impact of Social Media on Mental Health

A behavioral analytics research project uncovering how digital habits shape the psychological wellbeing of Gen Z — locally in Egypt and globally. Moving beyond assumptions to data-driven evidence.

Explore Insights → Explore Live Dashboard
About the Project

Why does this matter?

Social media use among Gen Z has become a daily constant — yet its relationship with mental health is often discussed through opinion rather than data. This project was built to change that.

We studied whether habits like screen time, doomscrolling, morning phone checks, and short-form content consumption correlate with increased anxiety, overthinking, mood instability, and disrupted sleep — across both Egyptian Gen Z and a global sample.

See Findings →

Research Question

Does social media usage correlate with measurable psychological strain in Gen Z?

Scope

Egypt (local sample) + global Gen Z behavioral data

Approach

Data analysis to discover the impact of digital habits on mental health

Outcome

Evidence-based recommendations for healthier digital habits

Sample Insights

Exploratory Findings

A showcase of preliminary correlations and sample behavioral patterns analyzed from Gen Z digital footprints.

01

Higher Usage → More Social Comparison

Increased social media use is strongly associated with elevated levels of social comparison behavior, affecting self-perception and satisfaction.

02

More Screen Time → Increased Overthinking

Higher usage rates correlate with measurably greater levels of overthinking and rumination, particularly in the hours before sleep.

03

Screen Time Raises Sleep Disorder Risk

Elevated daily screen time is positively correlated with higher risk of sleep problems — difficulty falling asleep, lower sleep quality, and reduced duration.

04

Sleep Instability → Mood Instability

As sleep disorder risk increases, mood stability decreases. Poor sleep and emotional dysregulation form a reinforcing cycle.

05

Morning Phone Check → Higher Nervousness

Individuals who check their phones immediately upon waking show significantly higher self-reported nervousness and irritability throughout the day.

06

Short-Form Video Drives Screen Time & Poor Sleep

Short-form video content (Reels, TikTok, Shorts) is the content type most strongly correlated with both increased daily screen time and sleep disruption.

Introduction

This study examined the relationship between social media usage and mental health among young adults aged 18–24. Researchers aimed to understand whether reducing social media engagement could improve psychological well-being using objective behavioral tracking instead of relying only on self-reported data.

Objective

  • Investigate how social media behavior affects mental health
  • Measure the psychological impact of a short-term social media detox
  • Identify whether problematic engagement patterns are more harmful than total screen time

Research Methodology

Phase 01 — Baseline Observation (2 Weeks)

Researchers objectively tracked participant behavior across major social media platforms: Instagram, TikTok, Snapchat, Facebook, and X.

Tracked metrics included:
  • Screen time
  • App pickups
  • Notification frequency
  • Daily mental health assessments using EMAs
Phase 02 — Social Media Detox (1 Week)

Participants voluntarily reduced their use of the selected social media platforms while researchers continued monitoring behavioral and psychological changes.

Key Results

Mental Health Improvements
  • Depression symptoms decreased by 24.8%
  • Anxiety symptoms decreased by 16.1%
  • Insomnia symptoms decreased by 14.5%
Behavioral Changes
  • Significant decrease in social media engagement
  • Slight increase in non-social screen activities
  • Increased time spent at home by approximately 42.8 minutes/day

Main Findings

  • The quality of social media engagement matters more than total usage time
  • Negative social comparison and compulsive engagement patterns are strongly linked to poor mental health outcomes
  • Participants with higher baseline mental health symptoms benefited the most from the detox intervention
Conclusion

Even a short-term reduction in social media exposure can produce meaningful improvements in emotional well-being, anxiety, depression, and sleep quality. The findings suggest that healthier digital habits may play an important role in improving youth mental health.

Introduction

This randomized controlled trial explored the psychological and cognitive effects of removing constant mobile internet access from smartphones. The study investigated whether disconnecting from continuous online connectivity could improve attention span, mental health, and overall well-being.

Objective

  • Provide causal evidence about the psychological effects of mobile internet access
  • Measure how constant online connectivity impacts attention and mental health
  • Evaluate whether reducing smartphone internet usage encourages healthier offline behaviors

Research Methodology

Intervention Period (2 Weeks)

Participants installed the Freedom app, which blocked mobile internet access, Wi-Fi connectivity, and mobile data usage. Smartphones could still be used for calls and text messaging. Internet access was still allowed through laptops and desktop devices.

Blocked connectivity:
  • Mobile internet access
  • Wi-Fi connectivity
  • Mobile data usage
Study Design

The experiment utilized a crossover randomized controlled trial with objective compliance tracking through the blocking application.

Researchers measured:
  • Sustained attention
  • Mental health indicators
  • Subjective well-being
  • Behavioral changes

Key Results

Mental Health & Well-being
  • Significant improvements in emotional well-being and stress reduction
  • Significant decrease in anxiety and depression symptoms
  • Effect size comparable to/stronger than clinical antidepressant interventions
Cognitive & Behavioral Changes
  • Sustained attention improved (comparable to 10 years younger cognitively)
  • Increased face-to-face social interaction and physical activity
  • Better sleep quality, more time outdoors, hobbies, and reading

Main Findings

  • Constant mobile internet access creates continuous cognitive distraction
  • Reducing online accessibility encourages healthier, active offline behaviors
  • Participants with higher baseline Fear of Missing Out (FoMO) experienced the strongest benefits
Conclusion

Blocking mobile internet access for only two weeks significantly improved attention span, mental health, and daily lifestyle behaviors. The findings provide strong evidence that constant smartphone connectivity may negatively affect psychological functioning and cognitive performance.

Actionable Insights

Recommendations

Evidence-based habits to protect mental health in a hyper-connected world.

Delay Your Morning Phone Check

Avoid checking your phone for the first 30–60 minutes after waking. Data shows this simple habit is associated with lower daily nervousness and more stable mood throughout the day.

Schedule Regular Digital Detox Periods

Deliberately allocating daily or weekly offline time has been shown in referenced studies to reduce anxiety, improve focus, and restore emotional regulation.

Reduce Short-Form Video Consumption

Short-form content (TikTok, Reels, Shorts) is the single strongest driver of screen time overrun and sleep disruption. Consider time limits or scheduled viewing windows.

Track Your App's Mental Impact

Actively monitor how individual apps affect your mood and anxiety. Use built-in screen time reports to identify patterns between heavy app use and emotional state changes.

Use Grayscale Mode to Reduce Compulsive Use

Switching your device display to grayscale removes the dopamine-triggering color stimulation that makes apps artificially compelling, reducing mindless scrolling significantly.
